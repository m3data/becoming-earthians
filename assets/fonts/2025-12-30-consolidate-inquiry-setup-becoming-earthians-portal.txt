  What makes this distinctive:

  Most resilience literature says "diversity good, monoculture bad" but stops at correlation. Your ABM identifies the mechanism: it's not diversity per se, it's whether agents have somewhere to go when they're spent. The relief pathway is the causal lever.

  That's a design-relevant finding. You can ask of any collective system: do the load-bearing members have independent recovery pathways? If not, you've identified cascade risk.

  The civilizational hook:

  This reframes debates about consensus, alignment, and coordination. The instinct under stress is to synchronize harder — but that's precisely what concentrates load and eliminates escape valves. The counterintuitive move is to protect difference as a structural resource.

> The civilisational frame: How can humanity cultivate adaptive capacity 
commensurate with the scale, speed, and entanglement of contemporary crises 
without collapsing into uniformity, control, or fragmentation? 

⏺ That's the question. And it names the failure modes precisely:

  - Uniformity — over-entrainment, everyone locked to the same attractor
  - Control — top-down synchronization that eliminates local agency
  - Fragmentation — no coupling at all, dissipated capacity

  Your work suggests these aren't opposites on a spectrum. They're all forms of the same failure: loss of relief pathways. Uniformity and control eliminate them by design. Fragmentation eliminates the coupling that makes them meaningful.

  Coherence is the third option: coupled enough to coordinate, diverse enough to have somewhere to go.

  Draft framing (for positioning document):

  Contemporary crises operate at unprecedented scale, speed, and entanglement. The instinct under stress is to synchronize — to align, control, simplify. But this is precisely what produces brittle systems: load concentrates on whoever remains different, and when they exhaust, the system cascades.

  This research investigates the alternative: adaptive capacity through coherence rather than alignment. Using agent-based modeling, biosignal instrumentation, and semantic analysis, it identifies the mechanisms by which collective systems maintain integrity under stress without collapsing into uniformity or fragmenting into noise.

  The core finding: what matters is not diversity itself, but whether load-bearing members have independent recovery pathways. Systems that preserve internal reference points — identity, place, practice — can absorb perturbation. Systems that optimize for consensus cannot.

  This has implications for governance design, collective intelligence systems, and the protocols by which humans and machines coordinate under uncertainty.
